弗雷格可能提出弗雷格之谜吗？

斯塔夫鲁拉·格雷扎科斯（Stavroula Glezakos）

在《余论》（Afterthoughts）一文中，戴维·卡普兰（David Kaplan）将弗雷格的《论涵义与指称》理论中的专有名词描述为“轻薄的”；然而，卡普兰承认该理论的核心承诺，他写道：“……无可争议的是，不同的专名具有不同的认知价值。”

弗雷格认为，包含名词的两个同一性句子，形如a=a和a=b，虽然可以通过同一对象的自我同一性使其都为真，但令人困惑的是，它们在认知特征上却有所不同。根据弗雷格的看法，形如a=a的句子是先天可知的，并且不传达信息；而形如a=b的句子则通常是具有信息的，不总是能先天地得知它们为真。弗雷格最终通过将这两种同一性句子之间所谓的认知差异归因于它们所包含的名词所表达的不同的Sinne（sense，涵义），从而解开了他的困惑。因此，弗雷格认为自己提出了一个先于并独立于特定的名称理论立场的谜题，并且用他的涵义（Sinn）与指称（Bedeutung）理论来得以解决。在本文中，我将提出，弗雷格所描述的是存在问题的。如果我们试图在不诉诸弗雷格理论承诺的情况下来描述真同一性句的认知状态，那么最初看起来令人困惑的问题大多都消失了。事实证明，为了产生谜题，弗雷格必须援引他用这个谜题建立所谓必要性的理论根据。

我的论据基于对卡普兰在他《词语》一文中提出的问题的严谨考察：是什么决定了包含名词的同一性句子的形式？如果一个同一性句子的形式可以在没有像“涵义”这种理论概念的情况下就得以确切说明，那么弗雷格将不能坚持说，形式为a=a的句子通常是没有信息的，或者是先天可知的（至少不比形式为a=b的句子更多）。相反，如果句中名词所表达的涵义的同异决定了同一性句子的形式，那么这个谜题就不再是一个普遍性的谜题：只有当我们假设弗雷格提出的理论实体的作用时，它才会出现。

1.真同一性句子的形式

弗雷格并不是通过实际的包含名词的句子来提出他的谜题，而是通过使用两个框架，a=a和a=b，来表示包含名词的同一性句子的形式。弗雷格通过列举这些句子所具有的认知特征来将句子归类于其中。最突出的是先天性：形式为a=a的句子“是先天成立的”；形式为a=b的真句子“并非总是可以先天确定”。但对句子认知特征的列举仍然没有回答一个基本问题：是什么使得一个同一性句子具有a=a的形式，而不是a=b的形式？

对于弗雷格而言，不合法的答案是：“是涵义的相同或不同决定了一个给定同一性句子的形式是a=a还是a=b。”这是不合理的，因为弗雷格显然在提出一个问题，这个问题可以在不依赖于他将为其提供解决方案的特定理论框架的情况下提出，并且其“困惑性”也是独立于理论解决方案的。我们应该首先感到困惑，然后才会意识到自己需要解决这一困惑。因此，弗雷格必须能够在不依赖涵义概念的情况下具体指出是什么让一个句子具有某种形式而非另一种形式——即使他最终将不同形式句子之间的认知差异归因于名词所对应的涵义的不同。

最自然的解释是，决定同一性句子形式的因素是名词的同一。没有任何理论上的承诺，我们可以说，当同一名词出现在同一性符号两侧时，句子有形式a=a，而当不同名词出现在同一性符号两侧时，句子有形式a=b。因此，中立地提出的（所谓的）谜题是：什么是包含两个同一名词的真同一性句子与包含两个不同名词的句子之间认知差异的根源？

当然，这种表述会立刻引发一个进一步的问题：什么导致了名词的同一性？

在《论涵义与指称》中，弗雷格对此问题的回答似乎是，当我们拥有相同的符号/指称组合时，我们就有了相同的名词。弗雷格的著名脚注B强烈佐证了这一观点，在那里他使用了“实际专名‘亚里士多德’”这一概念，并写道，由于“对于涵义的看法可能不同”，我们可以“容忍”同一名词的不同使用者之间对涵义的不同指定，“只要指称保持不变。”这一段使我们能理解，根据弗雷格的看法，什么是使得名词同一的条件，并引发了弗雷格理论框架内部的一个问题——是否同一名词可以与不同的涵义相关联，弗雷格的回答是：可以。

现在，既然弗雷格在名词个体化和句子形式的解释上提供了如此的说明，那么，为什么我们还要继续求索弗雷格的疑惑呢？我认为我们不应该：如果同一性句子的形式由它所包含的名词的相同或不同来决定，那么就没有理由认为形式为a=a的句子与形式为a=b的句子在认知上存在原则性的区别。

显然，一个人可能会两次遇到同一名词，并且否认包含该名词的同一性句子为真，或者在经过经验调查后才得知包含同一名词的同一性句子确实为真。“名词同一”这一概念并没有确保语言使用者——即使是最具能力的——能够在遇到名词时识别并重新识别它们，或将其纳入他们的词汇中。

当然，如果说话者在第一次使用或遇到名词“亚里士多德”后，然后在之后的某次中使用或遇到“亚里士多德”并认出这是同一个名词，那么（根据名词作为符号/指称组合的表述）她就能立即知道“亚里士多德=亚里士多德”是真的。但请注意，识别该名词同一的能力似乎涉及识别指称物是否相同。如果真是这样，那么形式为a=a的同一性句子原则上与形式为a=b的真句子在真理的判别上并无区别。在这两种情况下：一旦一个人知道指称物相同，就知道句子为真。如果他不知道这一点，或认为指称物不同，那么无论句子是形式为a=a还是a=b，他都不知道（更不用说先天地知道）该句子为真。

弗雷格自己在他的“亚里士多德”脚注中或多或少承认了这一点，尽管调用了他关于涵义和指称的概念。在那里，他指出，名词“亚里士多德”可能会被不同的使用者给予不同的涵义。这与承认同一个使用者可以为同一个名词给予不同的涵义十分接近了。尽管这些涵义决定了相同的指称物，但此人可能对此毫不知情。如果他在说“亚里士多德是聪明的”时，考虑的是亚历山大大帝的老师，而他在说“亚里士多德是希腊人”时，考虑的却是斯塔利亚的哲学家，他就无法立即认出“亚里士多德=亚里士多德”的真——至少不比那些将这些不同意义与两个不同名词（例如，“亚里士多德”和“Ari”）联系起来的人更好。

总之，我们不应该接受弗雷格最初的、所谓“令人困惑”的观点——形式为a=a和a=b的句子具有不同的认知特征。任何形式的句子的真都可以被完全胜任的语言使用者否定；此外，对于每个句子，认识到其真都需要认识到名词——无论是两个a，还是a和b——指称的对象是相同的。即使在援引弗雷格的框架之后，这个所谓的难题依然没有被解决。因为弗雷格将一个包含名词的同一性句子的认知状态与句子中名词表达的涵义联系在了一起。句子形式由名词的同一性决定，而名词并非（至少在《论涵义与指称》中）由所给予的涵义来个体化。因此，实际上没有理由从原则上赋予形式为a=a的句子特殊的认知状态——因此，也没有关于不同形式的真同一性句子之间认知差异的“谜题”。

2.“拉本医生”

弗雷格在他后来的论文《思想》中重新探讨了什么导致名词同一的问题，在其中他考虑了一种情境：两位朋友在讨论第三人困境。

弗雷格写道：

假设……赫伯特·加尔纳（Herbert Garner）知道古斯塔夫·拉本医生（Dr. Gustav Lauben）于1857年9月13日在NN出生，这一点没有其他人符合；然而，假设他不知道拉本博士现在住在哪里，也不知道关于他的其他任何事。另一方面，假设利奥·彼得（Leo Peter）不知道拉本医生于9月13日出生……那么，就“古斯塔夫·拉本医生”这个专名而言，尽管他们实际上都用这个名字指代同一个人，赫伯特·加尔纳和利奥·彼得之间也是语言不通的——因为他们不知道自己在做这件事。

在这里，弗雷格似乎向两个方向拉扯。一方面，呼应他在“亚里士多德”脚注中的观点，他写道，“古斯塔夫·拉本医生”这个专名可以被描述为一个独立于涵义给予的单称词项。另一方面，他显然认为，彼得和加尔纳没有意识到他们在使用同一个专名，是个疑难问题。弗雷格接着提出，应该将这个问题设定为：我们可以“……避免尴尬……”如果我们“……假设利奥·彼得使用专名‘拉本医生’，而赫伯特·加尔纳使用专名‘古斯塔夫·拉本’。”也就是说：弗雷格希望加尔纳和彼得使用的是不同的名称——可以这么说，他希望这一事实能够“表面上”显现出来。

显然，我们不能简单地“假设”弗雷格让我们去假设的东西。毕竟，他刚刚描述了一个情境，其中两个人都使用“拉本医生”。我们无疑希望理解在这种情况下发生了什么，在这种情况下，两位说话者使用“拉本医生”，在信念上却显然出现了分歧，以至于他们无法认出他们在说的是同一个人。

弗雷格试图通过声称加尔纳和彼得“……不说同一种语言”来解决他的困扰。这似乎等同于“拉本医生”（加尔纳口中）和“拉本医生”（彼得口中）是不同的名字。弗雷格为这一立场提供的唯一支持是加尔纳和彼得对拉本医生有不同的信念（也许还有他们都没有认识到这些信念是关于同一个人的这一事实）。本质上，这是认为加尔纳和彼得将不同的涵义与“拉本医生”相关联，导致有了两个不同的名称的“拉本医生”。根据这个观点，名词同一性由（至少部分由）相关的涵义来决定。

如果这是弗雷格的立场，那么我们必须重新审视这个谜题，并询问：既然弗雷格需要使用他解决方案中的理论工具来产生谜题，那么他真的能提出一个真正的谜题吗？这个难题是通过句子形式来提出的，而句子形式是由名词同一性决定的。如果这是（部分）由涵义给予来决定，那么为了产生困惑，我们必须从一开始就承认存在涵义及其在确定名词同一中的作用。如果我们抵制这一点，那么这个谜题就会消失。

3.结论

虽然弗雷格在《论涵义与指称》的开篇讨论中对真同一性句子的探讨曾被认为为所有语言理论家提出了一个问题，但实际上它并没有为任何人提供谜题。

正如我们所看到的，涵义在一个非弗雷格框架下，并未被用作名词个体化的工具，看起来指称物的识别将是识别名词的必要条件——因此，所有同一性句子将处于平等的认知地位。此外，即使在弗雷格的框架内，如果不同的涵义可以分配给形式为a=a的句子的构成部分，并且相同的涵义可以分配给形式为a=b的句子中的名词，那么弗雷格自己也不应认为不同形式的真同一性句子之间在原则上存在认知差异。

另一方面：如果弗雷格的真正立场是，包含名词的同一性句子的形式由个人语言使用者对句中名词分配的涵义来决定，那么结果就是，他的“谜题”和他的解决方案实际上是同一回事。只有那些认为涵义或名词使用者/解释者给予的认知价值在确定名词同一性中发挥作用的人，才能坚持认为形式为a=a的句子是先天可知的并且没有传递信息，而形式为a=b的句子则缺乏这些特征。但这种方式到底哪里令人困惑了呢？正是这一特征决定了名词在同一性句子中的同一性，并因此决定了句子的形式以及句子的认知状态。

最终结果是，非弗雷格学派的人不应该对真同一性句子感到困惑，因为他们没有理由接受不同形式的同一性句子之间存在认知差异。正如我们所看到的，弗雷格学派的人也不应感到困惑：因为正是他们的框架既产生了这种差异，也解释了这种差异。

英文原文

Can Frege Pose Frege’s Puzzle?

Stavroula Glezakos

In “Afterthoughts,” David Kaplan characterizes Gottlob Frege’s “On Sinn and Bedeutung” theory of proper names as “gossamer”;[1] nonetheless, Kaplan affirms the commitment at the heart of that theory, writing: “…it is indisputable that distinct proper names have distinct cognitive values”[2].

Frege maintained that two name-containing identity sentences, represented schematically as a=a and a=b, can both be made true by the same object’s self-identity but nonetheless, puzzlingly, differ in their epistemic profiles. According to Frege, sentences of the form a=a are knowable a priori and are uninformative; those of the form a=b are often informative, and cannot always be known to be true a priori. Frege eventually resolved his puzzlement by locating the source of the purported epistemic difference between the identity sentences in a difference in the Sinne, or senses, expressed by the names that the sentences contain.

Thus, Frege portrayed himself as describing a puzzle that can be posed prior to and independently of any particular theoretical position regarding names, and then resolving that puzzle with his theory of Sinn and Bedeutung. In this paper, I will suggest that Frege’s presentation is problematic. If attempt is made to characterize the epistemic status of true identity sentences without appeal to Frege’s theoretical commitments, then what initially seemed puzzling largely dissolves. It turns out that, in order to generate puzzlement, Frege must invoke the theoretical account that he uses the puzzle to establish the purported necessity of.

My case depends on taking seriously a question that Kaplan poses in his paper “Words”: what determines a name-containing identity sentence’s form?[3] If an identity sentence’s form is specifiable without appeal to something like the theoretical notion of Sinn, then Frege will not be able to maintain that sentences of the form a=a are, in general, uninformative, or knowable to be true a priori (at least: no more so than sentences of the form a=b). If, instead, sameness or difference in Sinne expressed by the names in the sentence is what determines an identity sentence’s form, then the puzzle fails to hold as a general puzzle: it arises only if we assume a role for the very sorts of theoretical entities that Frege presents it as leading us to.

1. The forms of true identity sentences

Frege does not pose his puzzle via actual name-containing sentences, but rather, by using two schemas, a=a and a=b, to represent the forms of name-containing identity sentences.[4] Frege places sentences in one or the other category by listing the epistemic features that they do or do not possess, most prominently, a priority: sentences of the form a=a “hold a priori”; true sentences of the form a=b “cannot always be established a priori”.[5] But the enumeration of sentences’ epistemic features leaves a basic question unanswered: what makes it the case that an identity sentence is of the form a=a as opposed to a=b?

An answer that would not be legitimate for Frege to give here is: “It is sameness or difference in sense that determines whether a given identity sentence has the form a=a or a=b.” This would be illegitimate because Frege is supposedly presenting a puzzle which can be posed, and has its ‘puzzlingness’, independently of the particular solution that he will go on propose for it. We are supposed to first be puzzled, and then find ourselves in need of a resolution of our puzzlement. Thus, it must be possible for Frege to specify what gives a sentence one form rather than another without making use of the notion of sense – even if he then goes on to attribute the epistemic differences between sentences of different forms to the difference in senses expressed by the names that they contain.

The most natural account of what determines an identity sentence’s form involves the notion of sameness of name. Without making any theoretical commitments, we can say that a sentence has the form a=a when the same name flanks the identity sign, and a=b when distinct names flank the identity sign.[6] Thus, posed neutrally, the (purported) puzzle is this: what is the source of the epistemic difference between true identity sentences that contain a single name twice, and those that contain two names?

Of course, this formulation immediately raises a further question: what makes for sameness of name?

Frege’s answer to this question in “On Sinn and Bedeutung” seems to be that we have the same name whenever we have the same sign/referent combination. That this is Frege’s view is strongly suggested by his well-known footnote B, where he makes use of the notion of “the actual proper name ‘Aristotle’”, and writes that, since “opinions as to the Sinn may differ”, we can “tolerate” variations in Sinn assignment among users of that name, “so long as the Bedeutung remains the same.”[7] This passage allows us to get a grip on what, according to Frege, makes for the same name, and then invites us to consider a question that arises within Frege’s theoretical framework – whether the same name can be associated with different Sinne – and receive an answer from Frege: yes, it can.

Now, why should we follow Frege in being puzzled, given this account of name individuation and sentence form? I suggest that we should not: if an identity sentence’s form is determined by sameness or difference in the names that it contains, then there will be no in-principle epistemic divide between sentences of the form a=a and a=b.

It is clear that someone may encounter the same name twice and go on to deny the truth of an identity sentence containing that single name, or learn only after empirical investigation that an identity sentence containing that single name twice is indeed true.[8] There is nothing involved in the notion “sameness of name” that ensures that language users – even the most competent – will be able to identify and re-identify names when they encounter them, or take them into their vocabularies.

Of course, if a speaker uses or encounters the name “Aristotle” on one occasion, uses or encounters “Aristotle” on a later occasion, and recognizes that the name is the same name on both occasions, then (given the characterization of name as a sign/referent combination) she can immediately know that “Aristotle=Aristotle” is true. But notice that the ability to recognize that the name is the same seems to involve the ability to recognize that the referent is the same. If this is the case, then an identity sentence of the form a=a is not, in principle, recognizable as true in a way that differs from a true sentence of the form a=b. In both cases: once one knows that the referent is the same, one knows that the sentence is true. If one does not know this, or believes the referents to be different, then, whether the sentence is of the form a=a or a=b, one does not know (let alone know a priori) that the sentence is true.

Frege himself more or less acknowledges this latter point in his “Aristotle” footnote, albeit by invoking his notions of sense and reference. There he points out that the name “Aristotle” may be assigned different senses by different users. This is a very short step away from recognizing that the same user could assign different senses to a single name. Though these senses determine the same referent, this person may remain unaware of that fact. If he says “Aristotle was wise,” thinking about the teacher of Alexander, and “Aristotle was Greek,” thinking about the Stagirite philosopher, he is in no way situated to immediately recognize the truth of “Aristotle=Aristotle” – at least, no more so than someone who has associated these distinct senses with two different names (for example, “Aristotle” and “Ari”).

In sum, we ought not accept Frege’s initial, supposedly “puzzlement-inducing” claim: that sentences of the form a=a and a=b have distinct epistemic profiles. The truth of a sentence of either form can be denied by a perfectly competent language user; furthermore, for each sentence, recognition of its truth involves recognition that the names – either a twice, or a and b – have the same referent. Even after invocation of Frege’s framework, the purported puzzle fares no better. This is because Frege links the epistemic status of a name-containing identity sentence to the Sinne expressed by the names in the sentence. But sentence form is determined by name identity, and names are not (at least, not in “On Sinn and Bedeutung”) individuated by assigned sense. Thus, it turns out that there is no principled reason to ascribe a special epistemic status to sentences of the a=a form – and thus, there is no ‘puzzle’ about the epistemic divide between true identity sentences of different forms.

2. “Dr. Lauben”

Frege revisits the issue of what makes for sameness of name in his later paper “Thought,” where he considers a situation in which two friends are discussing the travails of a third man.

Frege writes:

Suppose…that Herbert Garner knows that Dr. Gustav Lauben was born on 13 September 1857 in NN and this is not true of anyone else; suppose, however, that he does not know where Dr. Lauben now lives nor indeed anything else about him. On the other hand, suppose that Leo Peter does not know that Dr. Lauben was born on 13 September… Then as far as the proper name “Dr. Gustav Lauben” is concerned, Herbert Garner and Leo Peter do not speak the same language, although they do in fact designate the same man with this name; for they do not know that they are doing so.[9]

Here Frege seems to be pulled in two directions. On the one hand, echoing his “Aristotle” footnote, he writes of the proper name “Dr. Gustav Lauben”, specifiable as a single name independently of sense assignment. On the other hand, he clearly finds the fact that Peter and Garner do not believe themselves to be using one and the same proper name problematic. Frege goes on to suggest that the problem should be stipulated away; we can “…avoid the awkwardness…” if we “…suppose that Leo Peter uses the proper name ‘Dr. Lauben’ and Herbert Garner uses the proper name ‘Gustav Lauben’.”[10] That is: Frege wants it to be the case that Garner and Peter are using different names – and he wants this fact to appear “on the surface”, so to speak.

But of course, we cannot simply “suppose” what Frege invites us to suppose. After all, he has just described a case in which the men both use “Dr. Lauben”; surely, we want to understand what is going on in exactly such a case, in which two speakers using “Dr. Lauben” apparently diverge in their beliefs to a degree sufficient to prevent them from recognizing that they are speaking of the same man.

Frege attempts to resolve his worry with the claim that Garner and Peter “…do not speak the same language”. This seems tantamount to the position that “Dr. Lauben” (in Garner’s mouth) and “Dr. Lauben” (in Peter’s mouth) are distinct names. Frege offers as sole support for this position the fact that Garner and Peter have distinct beliefs about Dr. Lauben (as well as, perhaps, the fact that neither recognizes that the beliefs are about the same man). In essence, this is to hold that Garner and Peters’ association of distinct Sinn with “Dr. Lauben” results in there being two different names “Dr. Lauben”. Name identity, on this view, is (at least in part) determined by associated sense.

If this is Frege’s position, then we must revisit the puzzle, and inquire: could Frege really present us with a genuine puzzle, given that he would need to make use of the theoretical apparatus central to his solution in order to generate puzzlement? The puzzle is posed in terms of sentence form, which is determined by name identity: if this is (in part) determined by sense assignment, then, to be puzzled, we must at the outset be committed to the existence of senses and their role in determining name identity. If we resist this latter move, the puzzle dissolves.

3. Conclusion

Though Frege’s discussion of true identity sentences in the opening sentences of “On Sinn and Bedeutung” has been though to present a problem for all who theorize about language, it in fact does not present a puzzle for anyone.

As we have seen, in a non-Fregean framework, in which Sinne are not invoked as name-individuators, it seems that identification of the referent will be necessary for identification of the name – and thus, all identity sentences will be on an epistemic par. Furthermore, even within Frege’s framework, if different senses can be assigned to the constituents of a sentence of the form a=a, and the same sense can be assigned to the names in a sentence of the form a=b, then Frege himself should not hold that there are in-principle epistemic differences between true identity sentences of different forms.

On the other hand: if the true Fregean position is that a name-containing identity sentence’s form is determined by an individual language user’s assignment of sense(s) to the name(s) in the sentence, then what emerges is that his “puzzle” and his solution are in fact of a piece. Only one who holds that senses, or name user/interpreter assigned cognitive values, play a role in determining a name’s identity can maintain that a=a sentences are knowable a priori and uninformative, while a=b sentences lack these features. But in what way is this puzzling? The very same feature both determines the identity of the names in the identity sentence, and hence the sentence’s form, and the sentence’s epistemic status.

The end result is that non-Fregeans should not be puzzled about true identity sentences, for they have no reason to accept that there is an epistemic divide between identity sentences of different forms. Nor, as we have seen, should Fregeans be puzzled: for it is their framework that both generates the divide, and explains it.[11]
